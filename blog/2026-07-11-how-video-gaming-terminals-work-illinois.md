# How Video Gaming Terminals Work in Illinois (Plain-English Guide)

If you've walked past a café, bar, or truck stop around Champaign-Urbana and seen a small room with a handful of glowing screens, you've seen a video gaming terminal. Plenty of people play them without ever really knowing what's behind the glass — so here's a plain-English guide to **how video gaming terminals work in Illinois**: what a VGT actually is, who regulates them, how payouts are set, and where you can legally play in Champaign.

No jargon, no hype. Just the stuff worth knowing before you sit down.

## What is a video gaming terminal (VGT)?

A video gaming terminal is a regulated, state-licensed gaming machine that runs slot-style games and video poker. It looks like a casino machine and plays like one, but it lives in a licensed business — a café, restaurant, bar, or fraternal club — instead of a casino floor.

The important part: a VGT isn't a private arcade cabinet somebody rolled in off a truck. Every terminal in Illinois is licensed, tested, and connected to a statewide system. That's what separates a legal gaming café from something you should walk away from.

Two other things worth knowing up front:

- **You must be 21 or older to play.** No exceptions, and staff will check ID.
- **Illinois law caps how many terminals a single licensed location can operate.** That's why gaming cafés are small and calm by design — you won't find a football field of machines.

## Who regulates video gaming in Illinois?

Video gaming in Illinois is overseen by the **Illinois Gaming Board (IGB)**, the state agency that licenses and monitors the whole system. If you want to go straight to the source, the IGB publishes rules, licensee lists, and monthly reports at [igb.illinois.gov](https://www.igb.illinois.gov/).

The system has a few separate roles, and they're deliberately kept apart:

- **Terminal operators** own and service the machines.
- **Licensed establishments** (like a gaming café) host them.
- **Manufacturers and distributors** build and supply the equipment.
- **The IGB** licenses everyone in that chain and monitors play.

Every legal terminal also reports to a central communications system run by the state. That means the machines aren't operating on the honor system — play, wagers, and payouts are tracked centrally.

## How the games themselves work

Underneath the theme art, a VGT is a computer running a **random number generator (RNG)**. The RNG is constantly producing numbers, and the instant you hit spin or deal, it locks in an outcome.

A few consequences of that, which surprise a lot of new players:

### The machine has no memory

A terminal doesn't get "hot" or "cold." It doesn't know you've been playing for an hour, and it doesn't owe you anything. Every spin is independent. That's not a rule of thumb — it's how the math is built.

### Payback percentage is set at the machine, not the moment

Every game is programmed with a long-run payback percentage (often called RTP, or return to player). It describes what the game returns across an enormous number of plays — think millions, not your afternoon.

So a game with a high payback percentage still has short sessions that go badly, and a lower-payback game can hand somebody a great night. Payback describes the machine's long-run behavior, not your visit. Anyone promising you a "due" machine or a guaranteed strategy is selling something.

### Video poker is the one with a skill component

Slots are pure chance. **Video poker** is a little different — the cards you're dealt are random, but *which cards you hold* is a decision, and decisions can be better or worse. ## Payouts, tickets, and taxes

When you cash out a VGT in Illinois, the terminal prints a **voucher ticket** rather than dumping coins. You take it to the redemption terminal or the attendant, and you're done.

Two practical notes:

- Illinois sets a **maximum wager and maximum payout per play** on video gaming terminals — these are lower than what you'd see at a casino, which keeps sessions modest by design.
- Larger wins have tax paperwork attached. If you hit something big, staff will walk you through it.

The state and the local municipality both take a cut of net terminal income, which is a big part of why video gaming exists in Illinois at all: it funds capital projects. Your city gets a slice, too.

## Where can you legally play in Champaign-Urbana?

Anywhere with a valid IGB establishment license — which in practice means restaurants, bars, clubs, and dedicated **gaming cafés** around Champaign, Urbana, and Savoy. A dedicated gaming café tends to be the more comfortable option, because the machines aren't an afterthought crammed by the restrooms. The room is the point.

Here's what to look for in a good one:

- **Clean, well-lit, well-kept machines.** It tells you how the place is run.
- **Staff who'll actually explain a game** without making you feel dumb for asking.
- **A calm room** — not a hallway with a TV shouting at you.
- **Real hours,** so you can go after work instead of racing a 9 p.m. close.
